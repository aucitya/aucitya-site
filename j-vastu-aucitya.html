What Vastu Shastra actually understood about space

Vastu Shastra is one of the oldest surviving frameworks for thinking about built space. Its texts, some dating to the first millennium, describe in precise detail how a dwelling should be oriented, how its rooms should be arranged, and how the site itself should be read before a single wall is raised. For most people who encounter it today, Vastu is a set of rules: face the kitchen east, avoid a bedroom in the southwest corner, do not place a toilet here. That version of Vastu exists, and it is widely practised. But it is a long way from what the original texts were actually doing.

What Vastu understood, at its foundation, was that a building is not a sealed object. It is a site of continuous exchange between human life and the natural world. The orientation rules that now read as superstition were originally calibrated observations about the movement of the sun, the direction of prevailing winds, and the behaviour of groundwater across the subcontinent. The instruction to orient the main entrance east is an instruction to bring morning light into the first space a household enters each day. The guidance on kitchen placement reflects an understanding of ventilation and of how heat behaves in a room. These are not arbitrary prescriptions. They are spatial intelligence accumulated over centuries of observation.

The concept of the Vastu Purusha, the cosmic being said to inhabit every site and whose body maps to the grid of the building, is often cited as evidence that Vastu is purely ritual rather than practical. But the Vastu Purusha mandala, the geometric diagram that underlies the system, is also a remarkably sophisticated tool for reading a site. It maps the relationship between a building’s orientation, its internal hierarchy of spaces, and its relationship to light and air at different times of day. The cosmological language is the frame. The spatial logic inside it is functional.

The tradition also understood something that contemporary design frequently misses: the importance of the threshold. In Vastu, the boundary between outside and inside is not a line but a zone, a space of transition that allows the body and mind to adjust from one state to another. The verandah, the inner courtyard, the stepped well, the covered entrance passage: these are all threshold conditions, and they appear consistently in the buildings produced by Vastu-influenced design traditions across South Asia. The experience of entering a well-designed space, where arrival feels deliberate rather than abrupt, often traces back to this principle, whether or not the designer has ever read a word of Vastu.

The question of how to apply any of this to a contemporary apartment in Bangalore or Mumbai is a real and honest one. A fixed-plan concrete building has constraints that Vastu’s texts did not anticipate. Some principles apply directly: the orientation of a bedroom, the placement of a kitchen relative to a window, the creation of a genuine threshold at the entrance. Others require interpretation. The useful practice is to read the intelligence behind the instruction, rather than the instruction itself. A kitchen positioned for cross-ventilation is following Vastu’s underlying logic even if it does not match the cardinal direction the text specifies.

At Aucitya, spatial decisions about orientation, light, airflow, and the sequence of arrival are part of every project from the first site visit. The language we use is our own. The questions, how does light move through this space across the day, what does a person feel in the moment of entering, how does this room relate to the season and the sky, are among the oldest questions a designer can ask.

Questions Clients Ask

Practical answers

Do you require clients to follow Vastu?

No. Many of the spatial questions Vastu asks are ones we ask anyway: how does light enter this room, how does air move through it, what does the sequence of arrival feel like? When a client has specific Vastu requirements, we incorporate them. When they do not, the underlying spatial intelligence still informs how we read a site.

Can Vastu principles apply to a contemporary urban apartment?

Some apply directly. The orientation of a bedroom, the placement of a kitchen relative to a window, the design of the entrance: these are spatial decisions regardless of which framework you use to make them. Others require interpretation given the fixed constraints of an existing floor plan. The useful practice is to read the intelligence behind the instruction rather than the instruction itself.

What is the most important Vastu principle for a home, regardless of whether the client follows Vastu formally?

The threshold. The moment of entering a space, the transition from outside to inside, is one of the most consequential and most frequently underdesigned parts of any home. Vastu understood this and built elaborate spatial protocols around it. Even in a small apartment, designing the entrance as a genuine moment of arrival, rather than a direct step from corridor to living room, changes how the whole home feels to come back to.
