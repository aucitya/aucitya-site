Journal On Process

The client’s life as design brief

Every design project begins with a conversation. What most practices use that conversation for is to gather preferences: favourite colours, preferred styles, things the client has seen that they like. At Aucitya, we use it differently. We are not gathering preferences. We are trying to understand a life.

The questions we ask are not primarily about aesthetics. How does this household move through its mornings? Where does the quality of stillness need to be protected? What does this person need from this room at the end of a long day? Where does work happen, and what does the body need when it works? What does light look like in this space at 7am and at 9pm, and which of those hours matters more?

“The brief is never fully understood until the space is complete. But it can only be approached through the specific texture of a specific life.”

Why preferences are not enough

Preferences tell you what a person thinks they want. A life tells you what they actually need. These are not always the same thing, and a designer who only collects preferences will deliver a room that satisfies the first conversation but not the daily reality.

A client might say they prefer a minimalist aesthetic. What their life reveals is that they are surrounded by activity and noise for most of their waking hours, and what they need at home is profound material stillness, which minimalism can create, or can fail to create depending entirely on the specific decisions made. The preference is a starting point; the life is the brief.

The standard this sets

Designing from a life rather than a preference list creates a much higher standard of specificity. Every decision has to be justified not against aesthetic convention but against this person’s actual daily experience. Does this ceiling height serve how this person inhabits a room? Does this material choice account for how afternoon light arrives in this particular orientation?

The result is always entirely particular, a space that could not belong to anyone else, because it was built from the inside out. That is what we mean when we say Aucitya demands better decisions.

At Aucitya

How we apply this

The best briefs we have received were not the most detailed. They were the most honest. A client who says “I want a room that feels like the one I had as a child, without knowing exactly why” is giving us something to work with. A client who specifies twenty reference images without any sense of what they are looking for is not. We spend considerable time at the start of every project finding the real brief underneath the stated one.

The questions that get to it: what does this space need to give you every day? What is wrong with it right now? What will you do differently in this room than you do in any other? Those answers tell us more than any mood board.

Questions Clients Ask

Practical answers

What if I don't know what I want?

That is a perfectly reasonable starting point. Most people have a clear sense of what they do not want, and a feeling about what they do, without the vocabulary to articulate it precisely. We find that feeling first, and then work backwards to the decisions that create it.

How detailed should my brief be before I approach you?

The essential information is: what the space is, how you use it currently, what is not working about it, and roughly what the scope of work might be. Beyond that, we will develop the brief together. We do not expect clients to arrive with a complete design brief. That is our job.

What makes a project a good fit for Aucitya?

A client who wants the space to be right for them specifically, and who is willing to be part of the process of discovering what that means. The projects that go best are the ones where the client trusts us to ask the right questions and has the willingness to answer them honestly.
